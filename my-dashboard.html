How is this calculated?

The score weighs four things: how many breaches your email appears in, how recent they are, whether passwords were exposed and how they were stored (plaintext weighs most, strong hashes least), and whether sensitive data or stealer-log captures are involved. One recent plaintext exposure can outweigh several old, well-hashed ones.

AlienStealerLogs Stealer log 2026 · 299M accounts

A 23-billion-row collection of infostealer malware logs (ALIEN TXTBASE) traded on Telegram, holding credentials harvested directly from infected devices: browser-saved passwords, session cookies, and autofill data. Added to XposedOrNot in June 2026.

MiscellaneousBreached Feb 2025Plaintext credentials
Browser passwords Session cookies Autofill data Email addresses
What to do
  • Sign out of all sessions on your important accounts, then sign back in. This invalidates stolen cookies.
  • Change every password saved in your browser, starting with email and banking.
  • Run an antivirus scan on the device you use most. Stealer data comes from an infection.
Twitter 2023 · 200M accounts

In January 2023, a dataset of 200 million Twitter users was published on a hacking forum, compiled by abusing an API flaw during 2021 and 2022. It pairs email addresses with public profile details. No passwords were included.

Social mediaBreached 2021, published Jan 2023No passwords
Email addresses Names Usernames
What to do
  • No password was exposed. Watch for phishing emails that quote your real name or handle.
Canva 2019 · 137M accounts

In May 2019, the design platform Canva was breached, exposing account details for 137 million users. Passwords were bcrypt-hashed, which slows cracking, but unique passwords remain the only safe assumption.

Design / SaaSBreached May 2019Passwords bcrypt-hashed
Passwords (bcrypt) Email addresses Names Locations
What to do
  • Change your Canva password if you have not since May 2019, and anywhere else you reused it.
Adobe 2013 · 152M accounts

In October 2013, Adobe suffered one of the largest breaches of its era: 152 million accounts with encrypted passwords and plaintext password hints. The weak encryption plus hints made many passwords recoverable.

SoftwareBreached Oct 2013Weakly encrypted + plaintext hints
Passwords (plaintext hints) Email addresses Password hints
What to do
  • Retire the password you used on Adobe in 2013 everywhere. Password hints were exposed in plain text.
Dropbox 2012 · 68M accounts

In mid-2012, Dropbox was breached through a reused employee password. The full dataset of 68 million accounts with SHA-1 hashed passwords surfaced online four years later, in 2016.

Cloud storageBreached 2012, surfaced 2016Passwords SHA-1 hashed
Passwords (SHA-1) Email addresses
What to do
  • Old but reusable: if any account still uses your 2012-era password, change it and turn on two-factor authentication.
LinkedIn 2012 · 164M accounts

In 2012, LinkedIn was breached and the theft was initially thought to cover 6.5 million accounts. The full dataset of 164 million emails with unsalted SHA-1 password hashes went up for sale in 2016.

Professional networkBreached 2012, sold 2016Passwords SHA-1, unsalted
Passwords (SHA-1) Email addresses
What to do
  • Same as Dropbox: retire the old password and enable two-factor authentication on LinkedIn.
